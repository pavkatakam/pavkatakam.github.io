COGS 127 // DATA-DRIVEN UX/PRODUCT DESIGN

Pikmin Bloom Accessibility Redesign

Role

UX/UI Designer & Researcher

Timeline

March 2026 - June 2026

Impact

Redesigning AR gaming for the 1.3 billion people living with mobility impairments.

Teammates

Livianne Wong, Valerie Djatikusuma, and Neela Kolte

01. BACKGROUND

Inclusion is a $2 Trillion Opportunity

Most fitness-based AR (augmented reality) games are built on a single metric: footsteps. If you don't walk "normally", you don't progress. These games tend to exclude the global disability market, a demographic with about $2 trillion in annual disposable income.

The Problem

Pikmin Bloom's game interface relies on pedometer data and high-frequency tapping. For users with mobility aids (wheelchairs, walkers, etc.) or limited fine motor skills, the game isn't designed to accommodate them.

The Solution

We decided to pivot the game's philosophy from "Motivation to Walk" to "Motivation to Move." By integrating distance-based tracking and motor-inclusive toggles, we redesigned the experience to be universally rewarding, ensuring that no player is left behind.

02. USER RESEARCH

Behind the Screen

To understand the obstacles within Pikmin Bloom, we combined market analysis with firsthand interviews, including speaking with active users with current or past mobility impairments. We also consulted Steven Weitz, an accessibility advocate and game designer, whose advice helped shape our redesign.


Finding #1: "Step-Count"

From our interviews, we found that for users with mobility aids, the "step count" was often a inaccurate metric.

  • The Insight: Users reported that when the app failed to track distance moved due to a slower pace or mobility aid, it resulted in a feeling of discouragement.
  • The Change: We realized the problem wasn't just technical accuracy, but an noninclusive environment. This led to our "Motivation to Move" framework, ensuring that each movement is equivalent to a "step."

Finding #2: "Precision"

Through observation, we noticed that many of the game features of Pikmin Bloom (plucking petals and tapping small icons) require high-frequency, high-precision motor skills.

  • The Insight: For users with limited fine motor control, the repetitive tapping felt both difficult and exhausting.
  • The Change: We realized implementing larger interaction areas and "collect all" buttons will allow users with dexterity impairments enjoy the Pikmin Bloom game and community.

03. The Solution & Takeaways

The finalized system implements high-contrast structural hierarchy and dynamic UI elements. By connecting systematic design layouts with intentional user interaction pathways, we created an interface that reduces cognitive loading and optimizes overall task speed.
