Research Question

Do human predictions for total movie grosses and movie runtimes follow the optimal Bayesian prediction models for the power-law and Gaussian priors respectively?

Teammate

Christopher Arias

01. BACKGROUND

Heuristics

Most fitness-based AR (augmented reality) games are built on a single metric: footsteps. If you don't walk "normally", you don't progress. These games tend to exclude the global disability market, a demographic with about $2 trillion in annual disposable income.

A&A → BDT

Pikmin Bloom's game interface relies on pedometer data and high-frequency tapping. For users with mobility aids (wheelchairs, walkers, etc.) or limited fine motor skills, the game isn't designed to accommodate them.

Research Interest

We decided to pivot the game's philosophy from "Motivation to Walk" to "Motivation to Move." By integrating distance-based tracking and motor-inclusive toggles, we redesigned the experience to be universally rewarding, ensuring that no player is left behind.

02. METHODS

Replication

To understand the obstacles within Pikmin Bloom, we combined market analysis with firsthand interviews, including speaking with active users with current or past mobility impairments. We also consulted Steven Weitz, an accessibility advocate and game designer, whose advice helped shape our redesign.


Participants

From our interviews, we found that for users with mobility aids, the "step count" was often a inaccurate metric.

  • The Insight: Users reported that when the app failed to track distance moved due to a slower pace or mobility aid, it resulted in a feeling of discouragement.
  • The Change: We realized the problem wasn't just technical accuracy, but an noninclusive environment. This led to our "Motivation to Move" framework, ensuring that each movement is equivalent to a "step."

Questions

Through observation, we noticed that many of the game features of Pikmin Bloom (plucking petals and tapping small icons) require high-frequency, high-precision motor skills.

  • The Insight: For users with limited fine motor control, the repetitive tapping felt both difficult and exhausting.
  • The Change: We realized implementing larger interaction areas and "collect all" buttons will allow users with dexterity impairments enjoy the Pikmin Bloom game and community.

03. PROTOTYPES


Currently, Pikmin Bloom only has an integration for wheelchair users, which is limited to Apple Watch users (where one push equals 0.5 steps). This leads to leaving Android users and those without wearable tech excluded.

...since I walk slower than a 'normal' pace, the app doesn't always track my steps accurately... - 'C'


We decided to integrate distance-based tracking, as this will allow people with different mobility limitations to be able to have their exercise tracked accurately. A tool that can accurately track movement for everyone helps improve experiences for not only walkers or wheelchair users, but also runners and hikers, proving that designing for the few can improve the experience for many.


In addition, Pikmin Bloom’s current game structure requires specific tapping and dragging of objects, sometimes leading to a considerable amount of mis-taps. For example, nectar is fed to pikmin in order to increase their maturity to the flower stage. The original game play requires users to individually drag nectar in order to feed their pikmin.

…feeding my pikmin can cause the app to lag and sometimes the nectar doesn't drag to the pikmin I want it to go to… - 'C'


We decided to create larger create larger interaction areas, specifically a “bulk feed” button for feeding nectar to pikmin. In the new game play, users will be able to choose which type of nectar to feed their pikmin and the amount.

04. TESTING

We asked current Pikmin Bloom users to give us feedback...

We first tested our prototypes by asking users to find common game features from our newly redesigned home screen. Our users were able to easily find these features, leading to questions about the general layout and UI of the homescreen.


A new feature...

During user testing, one of our participants brought up the idea of selective feeding. The underlying issue with the original game’s feeding mechanic was that it required players to hold and drag their finger to feed multiple Pikmin. We decided to follow the game’s pre-existing conventions for multi-selection screens to allow the user to select which specific Pikmin to feed.


05. REFLECTION

Overall, this project helped me transition from basic UI knowledge to a production-ready Figma workflow, teaching me how to analyze and replicate an established company's design system.


What I learned...

  1. User research + testing: While my experience with interviews and data collection translated well for this project, I also learned more about specific tasks needed for user research and testing.
  2. Figma prototyping: Designing a Figma screen might be doable with minimal experience, but prototyping screens to transition between multiple screens was definitely a learning curve.

With more time...

I would love to work on this project with a different set of individuals: non-Pikmin Bloom users. We designed our project to be accessible for everyone, but with more user testing, we hope to make the interface intuitive for new users as well.
