COGS 108 // DATA SCIENCE IN PRACTICE

Who Can Afford to Go Green in California?

Role

Data Analyst & Researcher

Timeline

January 2025 - March 2025

Research Question

How does the average household income (per capita) affect the proportion of electric and zero-emission vehicles to total vehicles owned across California counties as of 2023?

Teammates

Livianne Wong, Valerie Djatikusuma, Stephanie Tam, and Christopher Arias

01. BACKGROUND

Specifically California?

California leads in EV adoption and policies, providing diverse data for analysis. While there might be some confounding factors, such as urbanity, it is common for most households to have some sort of vehicle. Many residents travel around the state to view the attractions and sights that California has to offer.

Covariates

Even with the interest that EV vehicles and programs are getting in California, each county has different local funding and revenue programs that can influence sales. In addition, certain areas have a lack of chargers or even markups in EV vehicle prices.

02. DISTRIBUTIONS

Dataset

We downloaded the resized DVM file, which has 84 different folders of different car brands (sorted by year and color) with images of the cars facing different angles. Using a random number generator, we chose 4 of them: Ferrari, Lotus, McLaren, and Rolls-Royce. This file also had an image table with image IDs, the predicted angle of the car in the image, and many more.


Code

From the CLIP_THINGS code, we ran the first 3 sections of code to use CLIP embeddings. After downloading the image_table.csv file, we cleaned the file to use the selected 4 brands and the left & right facing cars. The image table file had a unique image name for each image, so we had to make sure that each part of the image name was used when calling the images.


After cleaning the dataset, we used feature extraction to convert all our car images into a format that PCA and K-means can understand. Since we are using K-means for our project, we also found the ground-truth labels for this algorithm and used PCA for 10 components and 2 components.

03. DISCUSSION

Key Findings/h3>

Findings suggest a strong positive correlation between income and ZEV adoption. Wealthier counties, particularly in the Bay Area, tend to have more ZEVs, leading us to believe that ownership could be emerging as a status symbol tied to wealth. State incentives, infrastructure, and policy may also contribute to adoption rates. However, ZEVs still make up a small share of total vehicles.

Policy Considerations

ZEVs remain out of reach for many lower-income communities. We believe that California policymakers should expand incentives targeting low and middle income households to increase ZEV adoption. With increased ZEV adoption, more charging infrastructure will also be needed in underserved areas. Overall, future research is needed to assess the effectiveness of state-level policies and incentives.
