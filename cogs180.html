01. BACKGROUND

Heuristics

Human decisions use heuristics: mental shortcuts which allow humans to make quick decisions with minimal cognitive effort. One example is the anchoring and adjustment heuristic, where we are essentially basing our decision on the last number we heard associated with the topic. For example, when shopping during sales, stores will display the original price, as well as the marked down price. This will make the new marked down price seem like a bargain even when it isn’t, all because we are anchoring our decision to the original price.

A&A → BDT

Over time, the anchoring and adjustment heuristic has developed into part of the Bayesian Decision Theory. This theory is a model that involves updating one’s belief about some hypothesis as you receive more evidence or information. We all have a prior, but this prior can be changed with new evidence or an anchor. Combining our prior and anchor, we come to a new belief, called a posterior. We use this posterior to make decisions in our everyday life.

Research Interest

We decided to study to what extent everyday cognitive judgments follow an optimal Bayesian model across different statistical distributions. Specifically, do human predictions for total movie grosses and movie runtimes follow the optimal Bayesian prediction models for the power-law and Gaussian priors respectively? An observer is optimal if their predictions correspond to the posterior median of a Bayesian model using anchors and prior distributions.

02. METHODS

Replication

We plan to replicate the study on the optimal predictions in everyday cognition, by changing the study to relate more to current society. Out of the 9 topics tested, we decided to test our participants on total movie grosses and movie runtimes, topics we believed our participants would have some sort of prior knowledge on. In addition, we also only tested 3 values of anchors instead of 5 as our study was replicated on a smaller scale.


Participants

We were able to get a total of 31 responses from our friends and family. Our participants mostly consist of a younger crowd, 19-22 years old with some 30+ year old outliers. Every participant filled out a Google form, beginning with an initial unbiased question choosing a number 1-3, a number which determined which anchors they would be making their decisions on.

03. RESULTS

Data Distributions

Our dataset contains a collection of 1 million movies from the TMDB database up to 2024. Based on the given data, the two graphs show the empirical distribution of both movie runtimes and movie gross values. From the graphs, the movie gross distribution seems to be right skewed, with the majority of the data centered around 0 to 1 million dollars. On the other hand, the movie runtime distribution seems to be normally distributed but slightly right skewed. Based on the dataset and the graphs, the average runtime seems to be around 90 to 110 minutes.

Response Distributions

After receiving our responses and cleaning up our data, we created dot plots to match what Griffiths and Tenenbaum created for their study. We placed our given anchor values on the x-axis (30, 80, 110 for movie runtimes and 1, 10, 100 for movie gross values), and our participants' predicted values on the y-axis, as well as the median values for each anchor in red.

04. DISCUSSION

Movie Grosses

Our participants' responses suggested a skew toward higher values, whereas the actual TMDB dataset and the original paper's power-law prior showed a heavy skew toward low values (0–1 million dollars). We believe that our participants might have an "availability bias." The high values that the participants received leads us to believe that the majority of our participants only remembered high-grossing movies rather than the many low-earning films found in the TMDB dataset.


Movie Durations

Our findings suggest that human predictions for movie durations are consistent with Bayesian principles, as participants successfully used their prior knowledge of typical movie lengths with specific anchors to make realistic guesses. While individual predictions varied, the group medians tracked the optimal statistical "shape.” The median predictions remained slightly above the y=x line, showing that participants correctly used a Gaussian prior to adjust their guesses toward the typical mean.

05. LIMITATIONS

Sample Size

The biggest limitation of our study was our sample size of 31 participants. From these participants, the majority of our participants were in the 19–22 age range, leading us to not being able to generalize these results to the broader population. A larger, more diverse sample would be required to determine if the overestimation in movie grosses and accurate estimation of movie runtimes stays consistent across different age groups.


Generalization

While we cannot firmly conclude that the Bayesian Decision Theory and the anchoring heuristic exclusively control real world decision making, we understand that the significance of these findings goes beyond the world of media and entertainment towards critical real-world domains such as medical prognosis and emergency response. If human intuition is actually statistically valid, it implies that we possess an unconscious ability to make decisions by tracking environmental statistics.
